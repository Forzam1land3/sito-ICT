Impact and

Cyberstalking is not a "virtual" problem, but a real form of psychological violence, with real consequences on the mental and physical health of the victim and can be as harmful as in-person stalking.
Cyberstalking can have devastating effects on the mental health of victims. One of the most significant impacts is the onset of anxiety and chronic stress. Victims may live in a constant state of alert, worried about their safety and the possibility of being monitored or attacked.
This state of tension can lead to sleep problems, irritability and difficulty concentrating. Fear is another common response.
Cyberstalking can instill a pervasive sense of insecurity, making victims feel vulnerable not only in the virtual world but also in real life.
This fear can lead to significant behavioral changes, such as avoiding social media, changing daily routines, or even socially isolating oneself to reduce the risk of exposure to cyberstalker attacks.
Social isolation is a serious and often overlooked psychological effect. Victims may feel isolated or misunderstood, fearing that others will not take their concerns

This isolation can lead to depression and a reduced quality of life. The lack of support can also exacerbate feelings of helplessness and despair.
In some cases, victims may develop self-esteem issues, feeling guilty or responsible for the harassment they have suffered.
Even the body, even if it has not been physically touched, suffers the consequences of the emotional stress caused by cyberstalking.
Here are some common effects:
Fatigue and exhaustion: the mind is always active and on alert, and the body suffers with chronic fatigue.
Headaches, nausea and intestinal disorders: these are common physical symptoms related to anxiety and prolonged tension.
Panic attacks: in some situations, the victim may experience intense episodes of fear with symptoms such as tachycardia, sweating and difficulty breathing.
Let's not forget that in some cases, when pushed to the limit, the victim of cyberstalking may even go as far as to perform extreme gestures.

Typical Behaviours


Cyberstalking involves a series of behaviors and actions, over time, aimed at intimidating, alarming, frightening or harassing the victim and/or his or her family, partners and friends.
It can involve a series of behaviors, including, but not limited to, the following:

INAPPROPRIATE MESSAGES

Sending threatening or obscene messages or emails

Posting personal or sensitive information online

Victims may be constantly observed, spied on, and monitored by perpetrators, with or without their knowledge, on online and/or offline platforms. The behaviors and actions of cyberstalkers cause victims to fear for their safety and well-being, and depending on the cyberstalker's actions, this fear may extend to the safety and well-being of the victims' family, partners, and friends.

THE CYBERSTALKER

Definition

According to the definition contained in the Report signed by Janet Reno (1999), a cyberstalker is a computer harasser who uses e-mail or any other digital means to repeatedly harass and stalk another person.

Although they are usually intelligent people with sophisticated computer skills, in most cases they are socially isolated and emotionally immature, seeking attention and intimacy in cyberspace that they are normally unable to obtain in real life.

Among the most common cases, there are individuals who engage in cyberstalking behaviors against a known person: these are often ex-partners.
Then there are cyberstalkers who are infatuated with an unknown person, the targets can therefore be strangers or celebrities.

Extra

Psychiatrist, clinical criminologist and professor of Forensic Psychopathology, Vincenzo Mastronardi, the cyberstalker would be an emotionally undeveloped man who wants to be the protagonist, but at the same time is unable to relate properly with others. And behind a computer screen he becomes evil, inflicting pain on his victim.

THE VICTIM

Description

Age and gender are the factors most closely associated with the experience of online harassment.

A common element among the victims of such behaviors is a condition of weakness and inexperience, due not only to a lack of knowledge of the Internet and new computer media but also sometimes to emotional and/or physiological immaturity.

Not infrequently the victim knows the cyberstalker, linked to her by a simple acquaintance or by something deeper such as a friendly or sentimental relationship, a hypothesis in which the harassment begins precisely when this pseudo-relationship is interrupted by an explicit choice of the victim herself.

HOW CAN I PROTECT MYSELF?

Defending yourself from cyberstalking necessarily presupposes knowledge of the phenomenon, so as to acquire the necessary awareness to move safely in the world of the web and possibly react to episodes of harassment.
If you are a victim of cyberstalking, it is essential to act immediately to protect yourself and get support.

Here are some steps you can follow:

-Ask the harasser to stop
-Document the harasser
-Block the stalker
-Report the harassment
-Get support
-Remove personal information
-Limit online activity
-"Friend" only people you know in real life.
-Monitor your accounts
