What is Cyberstalking?

The Internet and technology in general today represent a fundamental aspect of social life, capable of modifying and permeating our daily lives in a total way. If the positive and democratic contribution of new technologies is undeniable, however the negative or even dangerous effects linked to the expansion of IT tools are inevitable. The presence of cybercrimes in people's lives is becoming increasingly more common.

There are many types of cyber crimes, and among the most common we can find Cyberstalking.
Cyberstalking is a form of online harassment that involves using the Internet or other forms of electronic communication to stalk or harass an individual.
This is a significant problem, destined to become increasingly complex and dangerous with the unstoppable increase in the use of the Internet and information technologies by ever larger segments of the population.

Real cases

BABY REINDEER

Richard Gadd, a comedian who was cyberstalked and harassed by a woman he met while working at a pub, has created a compelling Netflix series called “Baby Reindeer,” in which he explores the real world of cyberstalking through his own real-life experience.
Gadd met this woman in 2015 and kindly bought her a drink after she complained that she didn't have enough money to afford it.
From there, a disturbing escalation of harassment began: after managing to obtain his email, the woman stalked him for approximately four years.
During that time, Gadd received 41,071 emails, 350 hours of voicemails, 744 tweets, 46 Facebook messages and 106 pages of letters written by her.
Her relentless harassment exemplifies the invasive and destructive nature of cyberstalking, which disrupts every aspect of Gadd's life.
"At first everyone in the pub thought it was funny that I had a female admirer," Gadd told The Times in April 2024.
Then he started invading my private life by following me, showing up at my gigs, waiting outside my house, snooping on my social media accounts, sending thousands of voice messages and emails."
Although he went to the police to report the situation, the authorities were not very helpful. Gadd said they found it incomprehensible that a man could be stalked when the situation is stereotypically the opposite.
Now, despite the stalking having stopped, Gadd said the "emotional locks" continue to "live" inside him.

WALDMAN CASE

The campaign began in April 2014, shortly after Waldman's relationship with the victim ended, and continued intermittently until the date of Waldman's arrest.
Over the course of nearly four years, Waldman sent the victim hundreds of text messages, voicemails, and emails, and made numerous posts on multiple online platforms, in which he falsely claimed, among other things, that the victim was a drug abuser and had been diagnosed with bipolar disorder and narcissistic personality disorder.
Waldman also sent emails to the victim's employers, accusing the victim of being a "habitual drug user" and claiming that he would sue the victim for defamation, theft, trespassing, and other "illegal behavior."
During the four-year campaign, Waldman also repeatedly threatened to rape, kidnap, torture, injure, and kill the victim, to kill members of her family, and to commit suicide in her apartment.
As recently as 2018, Waldman continued to surveil the victim online and acquired tools that could be used to injure the victim.
According to Waldman's internet browser history, in 2018, Waldman tracked the victim's location, including his running path, and searched for telescopes, "pellet guns," "air rifles," and other similar devices.
Waldman was ultimately sentenced to 50 months in prison, while the victim was left deeply shaken and distressed by the incident and will have to live with the burden of this experience for the rest of her life.
